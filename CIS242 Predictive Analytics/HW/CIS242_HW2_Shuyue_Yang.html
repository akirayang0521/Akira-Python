Introducing Universal Bank¶

You were recently hired as a data scientist by Universal Bank. The bank’s Vice President is interested learning what predictive analytics can do for the bank. She supplies you with a dataset containing information on a sample of customers. Here is a description of each variable in the Universal Bank dataset:

  • Age: Customer’s age in years
  • Experience: Number of years of professional work experience
  • Income: Annual income in thousands of dollars (\$000)
  • Zip code: Zip code of home address
  • Family: Customer’s family size
  • CC Avg: Average spending on credit cards per month in thousands of dollars (\$000)
  • Education: Education level where 1 = Undergraduate; 2 = Graduate; and 3=Advanced/Professional
  • Mortgage: Value of house mortgage if any; in thousands of dollar (\$000)
  • Personal.Loan: Did the customer accept a personal loan offered in the bank’s last campaign? 1=Yes; 0 = No.
  • Securities.Account: Does the customer have a securities account with the bank? 1 = Yes; 0 = No.
  • CD.Account: Does the customer have a certificate of deposit (CD) account with the bank? 1 = Yes; 0 = No.
  • Online: Does the customer use Internet banking facilities? 1 = Yes; 0 = No.
  • Credit.Card: Does the customer use a credit card issued by Universal Bank? 1 = Yes; 0 = No.

3. What relationships would you expect there to be between age and other variables? Why?¶

In [ ]:
Experience: 
    Older people tend to have a greater number of years of professional work experience.
Income: 
    Older people tend to have a higher average annual income since they have more work experience and reach a higher job position.
Zip code: 
    No specific relationship. One zip code can have both expensive and cheap houses, so old and young people might live in the same area.
Family: 
    Older people tend to have a larger family size since they might have children and even grandchildren.
CC Avg: 
    Older people tend to spend less on credit cards since they do not shop much,
    while younger people might spend a lot.
Education:
    No specific relationship. Some older people might have advanced level since they have more time to pursue such degree,
    while others might only have undergraduate level due to previous backward education environment.
Mortgage: 
    Older people tend to have less or no mortgage since they now have enough money to pay off the mortgage they had at a young age,
    while younger people still face the mortgage problem.
Personal.Loan: 
    Older people tend to have fewer personal loans since they do not have financial difficulties,
    while younger people have fewer earnings and will accept a personal loan.
Securities.Account:
    No specific relationship since people can have securities accounts no matter what their age is.
CD.Account:
    Older people tend to have a CD account rather than young people since a CD account is a premium service and requires extra money.
    Older people can afford more on extra spending.
Online:
    Younger people tend to use Internet banking facilities rather than old people
    since older people might not know online technology as well as the younger ones.
Credit.Card:
    No specific relationship because people can have credit cards no matter what their age is.

6. Consider the zip code variable. What does that indicate? How might it be used by the bank?¶

In [ ]:
ZIP code indicates different areas. For example, 14627 covers areas in the UofR River Campus.
The bank can groupby different zip codes to see which area is the most valuable customer.

The bank can also use binning as a larger groupby to target a wider range of valuable customers.
For example, 146xx covers areas in Monroe County, a much wider geographical scope than UofR River Campus.
However in this dataset, since we do not know the corresponding area name of each zip code, it is difficult to use binning.

8. How might zip code be misused? What additional information or assumptions would contribute to such misuse?¶

In [ ]:
In America, there are many poor neighborhoods live right beside the rich neighborhoods.
One of the problem is that poor people and rich people might share the same zip code.
Since we try to groupby different zip codes to find valuable customers,
such circumstance would lead to ineffective data, for example, the standard deviation of income in one zip code is too large.

Thus, we might need a larger sample size to reduce the standard deviation of income for each zip code.
